To mandate or not to mandate? Vaccine Mandate Survey (For Research Team Only!)

Understanding Vaccine Mandate Features

Welcome! In this survey, we're exploring preferences about how vaccination policies might be designed in a hypothetical future pandemic (different from COVID-19), where safe and effective vaccines are widely available.

You’ll first learn about six key features of a potential vaccine mandate—who’s covered, the infection threshold that activates it, the percentage of vaccinated people needed to end it, whether incentives are offered, what exemptions might apply, and the personal or opportunity cost of getting vaccinated.

After that, you’ll see several scenarios where you compare two different mandates (A and B). For each scenario, pick which option you prefer, then indicate if you’d choose to have no mandate at all if such an option existed. Thank you for taking part!

Who Might Be Covered by a Mandate?

Scope of mandate indicates the group required to be vaccinated.

High‑risk occupations only: ⚠️ Targets those more likely to catch or spread the infection.

All occupations and public spaces: 🌐 Everyone is mandated to vaccinate, regardless of job risk.

When a Mandate Might Start

Infection threshold is the level of community transmission that triggers the mandate.

50 cases per 100k with 10% weekly increase: 🟢
Early trigger to keep infection levels minimal.

100 cases per 100k with 15% weekly increase: 🟠
Mid-level trigger, stepping in once the outbreak is noticeable but not severe.

200 cases per 100k with 20% weekly increase: 🔴
Late trigger, intervening only once cases have grown substantial.

When a Mandate Might End

Vaccine coverage requirement is the percentage of people that must be vaccinated before ending the mandate.

50% coverage: Ends relatively early, leaving more room for virus spread among the unvaccinated.

70% coverage: Ends once a significant majority is vaccinated.

90% coverage: Ends only when an overwhelming share of people are vaccinated.

Possible Incentives

Incentives include any perks or rewards to encourage vaccination.

No incentives: 🚫 People have no special support beyond public health info.

Paid time off (1–3 days): 🕒 Compensates for lost wages or personal time.

Government subsidies/discounts: 💸 Offers tangible support for getting vaccinated.

Who Can Opt Out?

Exemption policy outlines valid reasons an individual can forgo mandatory vaccination.

Medical exemptions only: 🩺 Strict criteria, requiring proof from a health professional.

Medical and religious exemptions: 🩺🙏 Broader possibilities for opting out.

Medical, religious, and broad personal belief: 🩺🙏💡 Most flexible, enabling many to skip vaccination.

Cost or Burden of Getting Vaccinated

Opportunity cost represents personal or financial burdens (transport, fees, missed work) related to vaccination.

No opportunity cost (A$0): $0 Zero burden.

Low (A$5): $ Small out-of-pocket cost.

Moderate (A$20): $$ Could deter those on tighter budgets.

High (A$50): $$$ Likely to discourage lower-income individuals further.

The Scenario

Imagine a novel virus causes a pandemic (not COVID‑19), and safe, effective vaccines are available. Governments worldwide consider whether and how to mandate vaccination. Each policy example you’ll see differs in scope, threshold, coverage requirement, incentives, exemptions, and personal cost—while the vaccine itself remains the same.

In each scenario, compare two mandates (A and B). Pick which one you prefer, then let us know if you’d choose “no mandate” if it were an option.
