To mandate or not to mandate? Vaccine Mandate Survey (For Research Team Only!)

Understanding Vaccine Mandate Features

Welcome! In this survey, we're exploring preferences about how vaccination policies might be set up in a hypothetical future pandemic (different from COVID-19), where safe and effective vaccines are widely available.

You’ll learn about six key features that can vary across mandates—like who’s covered, when it starts, what level of vaccination is required to end it, and so on. Then, you’ll see several scenarios comparing two possible mandate designs (A and B). For each scenario, select which option you prefer and whether you’d opt out entirely if that choice were on the table.

This survey is for research purposes only. Your individual responses will remain confidential. After completing, your answers will be sent automatically via email to the research team. Thank you for your time and consideration.

Tutorial: Who Might Be Covered by a Mandate?

Scope of mandate describes which groups or individuals must be vaccinated under the policy.

High‑risk occupations only: ⚠️ Applies exclusively to those with higher risk of infection or transmission.

All occupations and public spaces: 🌐 Covers the entire adult population, with no occupation-based limit.

Tutorial: When a Mandate Might Start

Infection threshold is the case level at which the mandate is activated.

50 cases per 100k with 10% weekly increase: 🟢
Early trigger, aiming to keep infection levels minimal.

100 cases per 100k with 15% weekly increase: 🟠
Mid-level trigger: it intervenes once the outbreak is visible but not extreme.

200 cases per 100k with 20% weekly increase: 🔴
Late trigger, stepping in only once case numbers are significant.

Tutorial: When a Mandate Might End

Vaccine coverage requirement is the percentage of people who must be vaccinated before the mandate is lifted.

50% coverage: Lifts relatively early, though many may remain unvaccinated.

70% coverage: Ends once a significant majority is covered.

90% coverage: Ends only with very high vaccination levels.

Tutorial: Possible Incentives

Incentives are benefits designed to encourage people to get vaccinated.

No incentives: 🚫 Individuals receive no additional support beyond general public health messaging.

Paid time off (1–3 days): 🕒 Helps offset lost wages.

Government subsidies/discounts: 💸 Offers monetary encouragement.

Tutorial: Who Can Opt Out?

Exemption policy identifies valid reasons someone might avoid mandatory vaccination.

Medical exemptions only: 🩺 Very narrow, medical proof required.

Medical and religious exemptions: 🩺🙏 Broader reasons for opting out.

Medical, religious, and broad personal belief: 🩺🙏💡 Most flexible, meaning higher potential for exemptions.

Tutorial: Cost or Burden of Getting Vaccinated

Opportunity cost refers to the personal or financial burden of vaccination—transport, missed work, etc.

No opportunity cost (A$0): $0 Easy and free.

Low (A$5): $ Minimal burden.

Moderate (A$20): $$ Noticeable hurdle.

High (A$50): $$$ Could deter many from vaccinating.

The Scenario

Imagine a future pandemic caused by a new virus (not COVID‑19). Safe, effective vaccines are available. Governments around the world are debating whether (and how) to mandate vaccination. Each policy example you’ll see uses the same vaccine but differs in how it’s implemented. You’ll compare two mandates and choose the one you prefer.

After making your choice, you’ll also tell us if you’d rather have no mandate at all if that option existed. Your answers help us understand which features make mandates more or less acceptable.
